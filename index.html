How does attention to others influence own reward-based actions?

The question of how to deal with the tradeoff between exploration and exploitation of options is omnipresent in our everyday life. For decades this matter has concerned experimental psychologists, game theorists and even neuroscientists. In 1979, John Gittins described a strategy for decision making processes related to probabilistic reward. However, this strategy only applied in certain, very constrained conditions: the reward rates for each option must be fixed, every option must be available any time, and the value of future rewards is exponentially discounted.

In this study, we investigate how using observational data from other people influences our own choices in respect to probability matching. Our simulations are a variation of Sugrue, L. P., Corrado, G. S., & Newsome, W. T. (2004): Matching behavior and the representation of value in the parietal cortex. First, we will summarize the aforementioned paper. Then we will motivate our variation of the study and describe our implementation. We illustrate the effect of different weights of others’ action related rewards and show in which cases how others’ influence on our own choices can be beneficial or unfavorable. Finally, we explain our next steps in developing this project.

  1. Matching behavior as investigated by Sugrue, Corrado and Newsome (2004)

In their paper, Sugrue, Corrado and Newsome (2004) explore matching behavior of monkeys. Matching behavior describes a matching of time spent at a foraging site to the probability of successfully finding food at that site in comparison to all other sites. More generally speaking, the fraction law says that the fraction of choices for a particular option will equal the fraction of total income due to that option. In this case, matching behavior was elicited in a two alternative forced choice task. Monkeys fixate a monitor in front of them which displays two (colored) targets. In each trial they choose either the red or green target. Both targets have (over a certain period of time) fixed reward rates. The rewards are assigned independently and stochastically via Poisson distribution. Importantly, the likelihood of receiving a reward from a specific target increases over time that this target has not been chosen. Sugrue et al. found neurons in the parietal cortex whose firing rate does not depend on reward probability per se, but on the reward probability in relation to a second target.

  1. Multi-player multi-armed bandits

Of the variety of research related to multi-armed bandits, only a very small fraction describes the problem in a setup with multiple players. Kripps, Keller and Rady (2003) introduce the idea of choice-outcome information for different options as public goods. In this scenario, players would prefer to keep choosing a certain (rewarding) option and let others take the costs of exploring new options instead. Liu and Zhao (2010) explored the behavior of multiple players who chose simultaneously and who have to share the reward if the same option is chosen.

While these studies investigate optimal strategies for exploitation vs. exploration in environments with several players and many possible choices, none of them raises the question about the influence of others’ actions on own actions with uncertainty if the same reward rates apply. The presented work wants to start this line of research via a simple simulation using an ideal observer.

  1. The simulation

The simulation was implemented using Python. It can be imagined as a turn-taking game with two targets, called A and B. The reward probability for each target is fixed but unknown by the players and may be different for each player. The choice probability for both players equals the player’s belief for income for each target. Both players start out initially with a choice probability of 0.5 for both targets. After each turn and action (choice) related outcome, the belief for income for each target is updated.

We simulated 2x3 (6 total) different scenarios. For each of these, the true reward probabilities for the two targets stays the same for player 2, the player whose action we are most interested in. These are 0.2 and 0.8 for target A and B, respectively. However, we use two different reward probabilities for player 1, the player whose action-related outcome player 2 might use when making their own decision. We simulate the case in which (1) the reward probabilities for player 1 are the same for both targets (0.6, 0.6) thus leading to a 1:1 reward rate, and (2) player 1 has the same reward rates (0.2, 0.8) as player 2 for both targets.

For each of these cases, we simulate three strategies according to which player 2 might take into account the observations of player 1’s behavior and subsequent outcome.

  1. Strategy: Independent
    Player 2 acts independent of player 1, i.e. does not take their action related reward into account at all.
  2. Strategy: 0.25 Weighting
    Player 2 uses information from player 1 to update their own belief of income, however weighs it less than their own action-related reward. We defined this weight as 0.25.
  3. Strategy: Joint
    Player 2 takes into account their own and player 1’s action-related outcome equally when updating their own belief of income.

  1. Results

We report the simulated behavior of player 2 which is (condition 2 and 3) or is not (condition 1) influenced by the action-related outcome of player 1. First, we illustrate the effect of influence through player 1 when player one has different reward rates than player 2. Second, we compare it to the case in which both players have same reward rates for both options. Lastly, we report the probability of choosing a certain option as function of its income rate.

Player 1 (0.6,0.6) has different reward rates than player 2 (0.2,0.8)

Figure 1 shows the belief in income over time for option A (left panel) and option B (right panel) of player 2. It is apparent that only when player 2 chooses independently of player 1 will they reach the true fractional income for option A and B, respectively. Player 1 has reward rates of 0.6 for both option, thus leading to a fractional income of 0.6/0.6=0.5. Giving the action-related outcome of player 1 weight, leads to leads to a belief in income and thus of choosing a certain option closer to 0.5 than 0.2 and 0.8, respectively.  

Another way of illustrating this effect of different strategies on choice of option A or B is via figure 2. The ideal matching behavior for player 2 and player 1 are indicated via grey (y=4x) and black (y=x) line, respectively. Deciding independently of the other player, leads to pattern of cumulative choices that closely resembles the ideal matching behavior of player 2. On the other hand, weighing both income histories equally (strategy 3) exhibits a choosing pattern closer to player 1’s ideal matching behavior. Strategy 2 shows an intermediate result.

Player 1 (0.2,0.8) has the same reward rates as player 2 (0.2,0.8)

Now we will explore the scenario in which the reward rate for both players are the same. Analogous to figure 1, figure 3 shows the belief in income over time for option A (left panel) and option B (right panel) of player 2. All three strategies converge to the same belief of income. Qualitative analysis of the plots leads to the conclusion that the belief of income reaches the true fractional income sooner when the other player’s actions and outcomes are taken into account. In the future we shall systematically test this hypothesis by averaging over many simulations and comparing the parameters of fitted polynomials.

Interestingly, the independent strategy exhibits a different pattern than both of the other strategies. Taking into account the other player’s action (weighted with 0.25 and equally), leads to a cumulative choice pattern close to the ideal matching behavior (figure 4). Choosing independently of observation of others does not reach the ideal matching behavior within the first 1000 timesteps.

Probability of choosing option A as function of income rate of A

The probability to choose option A (quotient of number of choices for A and the total number of choices) is (by definition) correlated to the belief of fractional income of option A. This belief in turn is linearly correlated with the true fractional income of A. Figure 5 shows the relationship between fractional income of A and probability to choose A. The independent strategy has the steepest slope, followed by 0.5 weighting and joint strategy. Notably, when the true fractional income for player 1 is the same as for player 2, all graphs meet, i.e. each strategy chooses A with the same probability. The further the true probability for the two players is apart, the further apart are also the probabilities of choosing A for the different strategies.

Figure 5: Probability of choosing option A as a function of the true fractional income of A. Two cases of fractional income of A of player 1 are illustrated: 0.5 (left panel) and 0.2 (right panel).

  1. Discussion

Which strategy to choose depends highly on the similarity of the reward rates for player 1 and player 2. It is striking that the strategies 2 and 3, which take the other player’s action related outcome history (weighed) into account, are advantageous when both players have the same fractional reward rates for both targets. Conversely, when the reward rates are very different, taking into account information from the other player is detrimental. This general idea is nicely illustrated in figure 5. Only the independent strategy (red) exhibits ideal matching behavior for any true income rate, both other strategies only do so when the income rate for both players are the same. The larger the difference between the two players’ reward rates, the more unfavorable is it to use the other player’s history when making reward-based decisions. This might seem obvious but has not been shown like this before to our knowledge.

  1. Conclusion and future steps

The present study uses a global observer to simulate choosing one of two targets by a player that has their own action-related outcome history as well as another player. We found that using information obtained from other players’ action-related reward is useful when their reward rates closely matches your own. Conversely, if they are different, it may be detrimental by leading to an unfavorable choice pattern. Further developing this idea of multiple players in a multi-armed bandit problem it is interesting to explore behavior based on hypotheses that the reward rates are the same or different. In this case there might be a ‘unfavorable valley’ in which the reward rates are just similar enough to keep the hypothesis that both players have the same reward rates, but not similar enough to actually benefit the choice behavior.

Furthermore, in next steps we want to introduce more than two targets and more than two players with some working memory cost of keeping not only your own but also others’ action-related reward in mind.

Finally, we would like to test this model on different populations of humans: People that do not know each other, people with a special relationship to each other like couples or siblings, and people with certain disorders, like autism-spectrum disorder.
